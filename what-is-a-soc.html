What actually happens inside a SOC?

Mavuso Williams · 26 Mar 2026 · 5 min read

Most people's mental image of a Security Operations Centre is a darkened room full of monitors, someone dramatically typing at a keyboard while red alerts flash across the wall. The reality is both more mundane and more interesting than that.

What a SOC actually does

At its core, a SOC exists to detect, investigate, and respond to security threats — in real time, around the clock. But that description flattens a lot of nuance. The work is fundamentally about decision-making under uncertainty.

Every day, thousands of alerts get generated. The job isn't to respond to all of them — that would be impossible. The job is to figure out which ones matter, as fast as possible, with incomplete information.

The alert queue: signal vs noise

Your SIEM — whether that's Splunk, Elastic, Microsoft Sentinel, or something else — is constantly ingesting logs and firing alerts based on rules you (or your vendor) have defined. A new analyst is often surprised by how many of those alerts are false positives.

Alert fatigue is the enemy. When everything looks urgent, nothing is.

Tuning detection rules to reduce noise without missing real threats is one of the most skilled parts of the job — and one of the most underappreciated.

The human side nobody talks about

What surprised me most when I started was how much communication matters. When you find something that looks like a real incident, you don't just work it silently. You document as you go, you escalate to the right people at the right time, and you often have to explain complex technical findings to non-technical stakeholders in plain language — under pressure.

That's a skill set that goes well beyond knowing your tools.

Entry-level reality check

Coming in as a Tier 1 analyst, the bulk of your work is triage. You're the first pair of eyes on most alerts. You learn to quickly assess: is this a real threat, a misconfigured rule, or just normal business activity that looks suspicious to the SIEM?

It's repetitive in places. But every few weeks, you'll work something genuinely interesting — a lateral movement pattern that almost slipped through, a phishing campaign that targeted your organisation specifically, a misconfigured S3 bucket that nobody knew was public. Those are the moments that make the repetition worth it.
